href="/wiki/Sinking_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="Sinking of the RMS Titanic">sinking of the RMS <i>Titanic</i></a></b> in the early morning of 15 April 1912, four days into the ship's <a href="/wiki/Maiden_voyage" title="Maiden voyage">maiden voyage</a> from <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>, was one of the deadliest <a href="/wiki/List_of_maritime_disasters_in_the_20th_century#Peacetime_disasters" title="List of maritime disasters in the 20th century">peacetime maritime disasters</a> in history, killing more than 1,500 people. The largest <a href="/wiki/Superliner_(passenger_ship)" class="mw-redirect" title="Superliner (passenger ship)">passenger liner</a> in service at the time, <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic"><i>Titanic</i></a> had an estimated 2,224&#160;people on board when she struck an <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">iceberg</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">North Atlantic</a>. The ship had received six warnings of sea ice but was travelling at near maximum speed when the lookouts sighted the iceberg. Unable to turn quickly enough, the ship suffered a glancing blow that buckled the <a href="/wiki/Starboard" class="mw-redirect" title="Starboard">starboard</a> side and opened five of sixteen <a href="/wiki/Compartment_(ship)" title="Compartment (ship)">compartments</a> to the sea. The disaster caused widespread outrage over the lack of lifeboats, lax regulations, and the unequal treatment of the three passenger classes during the evacuation. Inquiries recommended sweeping changes to maritime regulations, leading to the <a href="/wiki/International_Convention_for_the_Safety_of_Life_at_Sea" class="mw-redirect" title="International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea">International Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea</a> (1914), which continues to govern maritime safety.
